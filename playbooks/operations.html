Operations & AI FinOps

For: COO · VP Operations · FinOps Lead · Head of Infrastructure

The AI cost reckoning has arrived. Microsoft cancelled most of its Claude Code licences. Uber burned its 2026 AI tools budget in four months. The teams that build cost governance before scale avoid the incident. The ones that don't build it after.

Maturity Framework

Where does your function stand?

Four stages of AI operations maturity. The key distinction: visibility (seeing what you're spending) versus governance (managing it with the right model, attribution, and controls).

1
Aware

AI tool costs appear as a line item in your software budget. You review monthly invoices. You know the total spend on AI APIs and tools but can't attribute it to specific teams, features, or workflows. You're routing AI spend monitoring through the same dashboards used for traditional compute — which weren't designed for AI billing surfaces and will miss anomalies that traditional monitoring catches.

Tell: "We track AI costs in our software budget. We review the monthly total."

2
Piloting

You have basic API spend monitoring and alert thresholds set. You know roughly what AI costs per month and have some visibility into which models are used most. But you haven't yet tagged agents with owners and workloads, established cost-per-task baselines, or built anomaly detection tuned for AI billing surfaces. A compromised API key or an agentic workload that loops unexpectedly would still produce a surprise invoice.

Tell: "We have basic monitoring and alerts. Attribution at the task or agent level is still a gap."

3
Embedding

Every AI agent in production has an owner tag, a workload tag, and a cost-per-task baseline established before deployment. You have model routing: 80–85% of queries routed to budget models, 15–20% to frontier models, based on task complexity. Chargeback by team or feature is in place. Anomaly detection is tuned for AI billing surfaces separately from traditional compute monitoring. You can answer "which agent, team, or feature caused this bill?" within 24 hours.

Tell: "Every agent has an owner and baseline. Model routing is running. We can attribute any cost."

4
Leading

AI FinOps is a distinct governance layer with its own anomaly model, attribution model, and credit/cap policies. You run model routing and compute optimization continuously — FP8 quantization, KV cache reuse, spot GPU pricing for interruptible workloads, idle GPU shutdown policies. Engineers have direct ownership of cost outcomes for their features. Your effective AI infrastructure cost is 40–70% lower than teams running equivalent workloads without this governance layer.

Tell: "AI FinOps is a governance layer, not a dashboard. Engineers own their cost outcomes."

Priority Actions

Your next move

Locate your maturity stage above, then focus on the actions that move you to the next one.

Stage 1 → 2  ·  Aware to Piloting
  • Tag every AI agent in production with an owner, workload, and monthly cost estimate. Do this today — before the next invoice, not after.
  • Build anomaly detection tuned for AI billing separately from your existing cloud monitoring. Set tiered alerts at 50%, 80%, and 95% of monthly AI budget. Standard cloud monitoring won't catch AI billing anomalies at the rate they occur.
  • Stop measuring AI adoption by consumption metrics. Token counts and tool usage rates measure the wrong thing. Identify two outcome metrics you can track instead.
Stage 2 → 3  ·  Piloting to Embedding
  • Establish cost-per-task baselines for every agent before it scales. The baseline is the reference point for catching drift — you can't govern what you haven't measured.
  • Implement model routing: route 80–85% of queries to a budget model, 15–20% to frontier. For most production systems, this split is achievable with under 2% quality loss and 40–60% cost reduction.
  • Implement chargeback by team or feature. When engineering teams own their AI cost outcomes directly, the governance problem largely solves itself — the incentive is aligned with the outcome.
Stage 3 → 4  ·  Embedding to Leading
  • Run the full GPU FinOps playbook: FP8 quantization for H100 workloads, KV cache reuse for long-context and multi-turn agents, spot GPU pricing for training and interruptible inference jobs, idle GPU shutdown policies. These are independent savings layers — implement in order of ease.
  • Build approval workflows for high-cost inference calls. Agentic workloads that consume 100× more tokens than standard queries need human gates before they run at scale, not after they produce an incident.
  • Treat AI FinOps as a separate governance discipline with dedicated ownership. If no one person is accountable for AI cost outcomes across your engineering organisation, the governance layer doesn't exist — it's just a dashboard nobody acts on.
