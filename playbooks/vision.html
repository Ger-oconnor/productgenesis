Vision & Product Strategy

For: CPO · VP Product · Head of Product Design · Senior PM

Speed of execution is now table stakes. Strategic judgment — choosing what matters and making it mean something — is the only edge AI doesn't compress.

Maturity Framework

Where does your function stand?

Four stages of AI maturity for product leaders. Read each and locate yourself honestly — not aspirationally.

1
Aware

Your team uses AI tools individually — faster first drafts, research summaries, meeting notes, spec generation. AI makes people more productive but hasn't changed how the product function operates. Your roadmap process, discovery methodology, and decision-making structure are the same as they were two years ago.

Tell: "We've added AI tools to our PM workflow and saved time on admin tasks."

2
Piloting

You've shipped AI features and started redesigning some discovery workflows around AI synthesis. Cycle times have improved. PMs are using AI earlier in the discovery process. But the split between "product that uses AI" and "product that is AI" hasn't been addressed at the strategic level — your product still has AI as a feature layer, not as its core.

Tell: "We've shipped AI features and our PMs use AI in discovery. Cycles are faster."

3
Embedding

Your product strategy process has been redesigned around AI, not just assisted by it. You've articulated where your product is "AI as the product" versus "AI as a feature" and made architectural decisions accordingly. Agentic playbooks run in discovery and development. You measure outcomes, not AI tool adoption. Execution has been handed to AI; judgment is what PMs bring.

Tell: "Our PMs focus on judgment and strategy. Execution throughput is AI's job."

4
Leading

Product strategy and AI strategy are indistinguishable. Your product is AI — the intelligence layer is the experience, not a feature layered onto it. You're designing for generative UI, questioning every static information architecture, and building toward interfaces that adapt at runtime. Your PMs are strategic architects who think in systems, not in features.

Tell: "Our product is AI. Strategy and AI strategy are one conversation."

Frontier Signals

Where best practice is moving

Synthesised from the most recent and impactful content in the Vision category.

Three AI shockwaves broke the classical PM playbook — velocity compression, machine-speed behaviour change, and autonomous learning loops.

2026 is the first year when product management must consciously evolve beyond the classical skill set — not use AI to do the classical job faster, but actually evolve. Engineering velocity compression means features ship in days, not quarters: the planning horizon and the cadence of rituals must change with it. User behaviour now changes at machine speed, making quarterly research and biannual roadmaps structurally too slow. And autonomous product learning loops — features that self-modify based on usage without PM initiation — turn the PM role from decision-maker to governance-setter. The teams running the 2022 playbook in 2026 are running the wrong playbook.

Source: LogRocket Blog
Error cases in AI design tools have almost entirely stopped happening. The experimental phase is over — this is the baseline now.

What Onlook observes in May 2026 is that the frustrating failures that made AI-assisted design feel experimental have almost entirely stopped. Agent workflows have matured. Models provide more reliable output. Designers have learned what it is reasonable to ask for. The implication for designers is structural: when the canvas is sourced in code by default, the design medium has changed. The question is no longer whether AI can help — it is whether a designer's judgment, taste, and craft can stay ahead of what an LLM will suggest as the obvious first answer. Designers who were trading on their ability to produce "obvious" answers have less professional edge than they had twelve months ago.

Source: Onlook · Substack
The AI-native designer's job has changed: from making to deciding. Discovering what to build is now more valuable than building it.

An AI-native designer isn't someone who uses AI tools faster — it's someone who has recognised that the design work worth doing has changed. When AI produces solid starting points and sometimes final results, producing the right prototype (the one that tests the right assumption, frames the right question) is where the work is. AI-native designers spend more time on problem framing, use AI to generate options rapidly, and treat evaluation as the work. The generation is just the means. Designers who haven't made this mental model switch are producing faster work but not better work — and in 2026, faster is not the edge.

Source: Verified Insider · Substack
Agentic AI didn't just add tools to the PM's job — it changed which tasks are worth doing at all.

The product manager's job in 2026 has not just gained new tools — it has changed in structure. Agentic AI compresses the cost of synthesis, competitive analysis, first-draft documentation, and routine data analysis to near zero. This does not make PMs more productive at the same job — it changes which parts of the job constitute the actual work. What remains high-judgment is the set of decisions where AI gives confident, plausible, and wrong answers: strategic prioritisation, stakeholder navigation, and the evaluation of AI-generated outputs. PMs who haven't updated their task list are running a stale job description.

Source: Next Gen Product Manager
Priority Actions

Your next move

Locate your maturity stage above, then focus on the actions that move you to the next one.

Stage 1 → 2  ·  Aware to Piloting
  • Audit your team's AI usage. Separate "faster output" (execution throughput) from "better decisions" (strategic judgment). Most teams are almost entirely in the first category.
  • Run at least one product discovery process redesigned around AI synthesis — not assisted by it. Can AI handle the research synthesis? What does the PM actually add when it does?
  • Set a cycle time benchmark now. You need a baseline before you can measure the change that agentic playbooks create.
Stage 2 → 3  ·  Piloting to Embedding
  • Define explicitly what "product that is AI" means for your specific product — not as a positioning statement, but as an architectural decision. What changes about the interface, the data model, the delivery mechanism?
  • Redesign at least one PM role around strategic judgment, not execution throughput. What does that PM's day look like when AI handles the artefacts?
  • Establish agentic playbooks for discovery and requirements. Target 10-day cycles compressing to 3–4 days within two quarters.
Stage 3 → 4  ·  Embedding to Leading
  • Question every static information architecture in your product. Which interfaces should adapt at runtime? Start with one surface and design for generative UI.
  • Review your product strategy and AI strategy. Are they the same document or two separate conversations? If separate, that's the gap to close.
  • Map your team's skill profile. How many of your senior PMs are strategic architects who think in systems? That's the capability to grow — not execution throughput.
