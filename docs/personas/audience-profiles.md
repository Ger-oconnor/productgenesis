# Product Genesis — Audience Profiles

**Version:** 1.0  
**Date:** 2026-05-21  
**Purpose:** Reference document for content strategy, story framing, and writing tone. Load as context when drafting posts.  
**Personas:** Two named individuals representing the two primary reader archetypes.

---

## Part 1 — Empathy Map

One shared map. Split lines show where C-suite and Function Heads diverge.

### Context

| Dimension | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Persona A** | C-suite: CEO, COO, CDO, CTO |
| **Persona B** | Function heads: CPO, VP Product, VP Engineering |
| **Org size** | Enterprise (1,000+) and Mid-market (100–1,000) |
| **Primary trigger** | Fear of falling behind — competitors shipping AI, board asking questions |
| **Primary info sources** | LinkedIn, newsletters, McKinsey reports, a16z posts, curated exec feeds |

### THINK & FEEL
*Inner emotional world — what occupies their mind beneath the surface*

**Shared:**
- "Am I making the right bets, or will I look foolish in 18 months?"
- Anxiety that AI will expose capability gaps in the organisation before they've had time to fix them
- Pride at stake — their reputation is built on reading markets correctly

**C-suite:**
- Guilt about not yet having a coherent AI narrative for the board
- Fear of being outmaneuvered by a competitor who moves faster *and* smarter

**Function heads:**
- "My team is looking to me for direction and I don't fully have it yet"
- Caught between executive pressure to "do AI" and the engineering/product reality of what's actually deliverable
- Fear of shipping something that embarrasses the company or locks them into the wrong stack

### SEE
*What they observe in their environment — media, peers, org*

**Shared:**
- Competitors announcing AI features — even superficial ones get press
- LinkedIn and inboxes full of AI transformation stories where it's impossible to separate signal from hype
- New models and tools arriving faster than they can evaluate them
- Their own teams running ad hoc AI experiments without coordination

**C-suite:**
- Board decks asking: "What is our AI strategy?"
- Other CEOs at conferences claiming their transformation is ahead of schedule

**Function heads:**
- Engineers who want to use every new tool the moment it ships
- Product backlogs filling with AI feature requests nobody has prioritised
- Junior PMs and engineers who know more about the latest models than they do

### HEAR
*What they're being told — by board, team, peers, media*

**Shared:**
- "We need to move faster on AI"
- "What's our moat once everyone has access to the same models?"
- Contradictory advice from every direction: build vs. buy, which model, which use case first
- "Everything changed again" — every major model release resets the conversation

**C-suite:**
- Board: "What are we doing about AI?" — asked more urgently each quarter
- Investors: "Your competitors just raised for an AI play"
- Advisors and consultants: high-level frameworks that don't translate into decisions
- Peers at conferences: confident-sounding takes that feel hollow on closer inspection

**Function heads:**
- Engineers: "We should be using the latest model/tool"
- Their boss: "Why aren't we shipping AI features yet?"
- Customers: mixed signals — some excited, some skeptical, most just waiting to see
- Vendors: every demo looks impressive, none of them look like production

### SAY & DO
*What they say publicly and how they behave — including what they won't admit*

**Shared:**
- SAY: "AI is a top strategic priority for us this year"
- DO: Attend AI summits, roundtables, and briefings — actively hunting for clarity
- DON'T: Admit openly that they're uncertain or behind

**C-suite:**
- SAY: "We're evaluating our options carefully" — often a stall
- DO: Commission a task force or bring in consultants to buy time and cover
- DO: Make a symbolic AI hire — Chief AI Officer — to signal intent to the market

**Function heads:**
- SAY: "We're being deliberate about where AI adds real value"
- DO: Run safe, low-visibility experiments to generate something to show
- DO: Build business cases upward while quietly managing team expectations downward
- DON'T: Push back visibly on executive AI mandates even when privately skeptical

### PAINS
*Fears, frustrations, and obstacles*

**Shared:**
- Information overload with no signal — new models, tools, and vendors arrive faster than they can evaluate
- No clear framework for prioritising which AI use cases to pursue first
- Impossible to tell real competitive AI moves from marketing theatre
- Speed pressure creates shortcuts that accumulate as technical and organisational debt

**C-suite:**
- Making a large public commitment before the strategy is solid
- Not knowing enough to evaluate what their own teams are telling them
- Board and investor credibility on the line if AI bets don't pay off
- Organisational resistance when pushing AI transformation downward

**Function heads:**
- Blamed for slow execution when the real problem is unclear direction from above
- Vendor lock-in from rushed decisions made under pressure
- Teams burning out on AI experiments that never ship
- Gap between what models can do in demos vs. in production at scale
- Difficulty measuring ROI in a way that satisfies finance

### GAINS
*What success looks and feels like*

**Shared:**
- A clear, defensible point of view on where AI creates value in their specific business
- Confidence to speak credibly about AI — to board, team, press, and peers
- A prioritisation framework that doesn't require tracking every new development

**C-suite:**
- A narrative: "Here's where we're going and why" — for investors, board, and press
- Visible, credible progress that doesn't bet the company
- Being seen as the leader who "got AI right" while peers stumbled or overcorrected

**Function heads:**
- A clear mandate from above with enough room to execute
- Concrete decision criteria: build vs. buy, which use cases first
- Something real shipped — evidence-based wins to point to
- Team retained and motivated, not poached by AI-native companies
- A way to translate executive pressure into a roadmap that's actually deliverable

## Part 2 — Persona A: The C-Suite Leader

### Marcus Reilly
**Title:** CEO  
**Company:** Vantara — B2B enterprise workforce management software, ~1,800 employees  
**Age:** 51  
**Location:** Chicago, IL  
**Tenure as CEO:** 4 years  

### Background
Marcus spent 14 years at McKinsey before joining Vantara as COO, then stepping into the CEO role. He is operationally sharp, commercially astute, and used to being the most prepared person in the room. He built his reputation on seeing around corners — identifying market shifts before competitors and repositioning accordingly. AI is the first trend in a decade where he genuinely doesn't feel ahead of the curve, and it bothers him more than he lets on.

Vantara has 120 engineers, a legacy SaaS platform, and a customer base of large enterprises with 5–7 year contracts. The board has started asking about AI in every quarterly review. Two of his largest competitors have announced AI-powered features. One of them is getting press.

### Goals & Motivations
- Maintain Vantara's market position through the AI transition without making a bet that backfires publicly
- Build a credible AI narrative for the board and investors before the next quarterly review
- Be the kind of CEO who "got AI right" — seen as thoughtful, not reckless, not slow
- Retain key talent who are being aggressively recruited by AI-native companies

### A Day in Marcus's Life
**6:15am** — Scrolls LinkedIn in bed before getting up. Saves four articles. Reads none of them.  
**8:30am** — Board prep with COO and CFO. Someone mentions a competitor just launched an AI scheduling feature. Marcus writes a note: *"Ask Priya about this."*  
**10:00am** — Investor call. Gets asked about AI roadmap. Gives the "we're being strategic and deliberate" answer. Feels the hollowness of it as he says it.  
**12:30pm** — Lunch with a peer CEO. They spend 20 minutes trading AI vendor horror stories and comparing notes on consultant pitches. Neither has made a move yet.  
**2:00pm** — Vendor demo for an AI-powered analytics layer. The demo is impressive. Marcus asks: "What does implementation look like?" and "Who else in our industry is using this in production?" Neither answer satisfies him.  
**5:15pm** — Reads a McKinsey report on his flight home. Highlights half of it. Thinks "this is useful" but can't extract a single specific action from it.  
**9:00pm** — Sends his CPO a LinkedIn article with the message: *"Thoughts?"*

### Information Diet
- **Daily:** LinkedIn (scrolls feed, saves more than reads), WSJ, Axios Pro
- **Weekly:** Stratechery (Ben Thompson), McKinsey newsletter, HBR digest
- **Events:** SaaStr Annual, Gartner IT Symposium, CEO peer roundtables
- **Trusted voices:** 3–4 peer CEOs he calls when making big decisions; his board's technology advisor
- **How he consumes:** Primarily text; audio on commutes (podcasts: Acquired, All-In, Lex Fridman occasionally)

### Decision-Making Style
Marcus is a consensus-builder before he moves. He polls his trusted peer network, asks his leadership team to pressure-test assumptions, then acts decisively once he has conviction. He avoids being first but hates being last. He is suspicious of hype and responds better to concrete case studies than to frameworks or predictions.

### Relationship with AI — Current State
- Has approved two internal AI experiments but hasn't personally tracked their progress
- Gets briefed by his CTO monthly; finds the briefings either too technical or too vague
- Has read enough to know the vocabulary but not enough to evaluate claims critically
- Privately concerned that his most talented engineers are looking at AI-native startups

### What He Needs from Content
- Concrete examples of what companies like Vantara are actually doing — not "a Fortune 500 company" but something specific enough to be credible
- Decision frameworks that fit a legacy SaaS context, not a greenfield startup
- Permission to be deliberate without being told he's falling behind
- The kind of insight he can quote in a board presentation without having to verify it

### Content Triggers — What Makes Him Click
- A specific company he recognises made a move he didn't know about
- A stat that reframes the risk in a way he hasn't considered
- A title that sounds like it was written for his exact situation
- A peer he respects shares it

### Content Killers — What Makes Him Bounce
- Generic "AI is transforming everything" framing
- Startup-first perspective with no acknowledgement of legacy complexity
- More than two paragraphs before he understands the point
- Anything that reads like a vendor wrote it

### Signature Quote
*"I don't need to understand how the engine works. I need to know which road to take and why."*

## Part 3 — Persona B: The Function Head

### Priya Sharma
**Title:** Chief Product Officer  
**Company:** Clarent — B2B fintech, payment infrastructure for mid-market retail, ~380 employees  
**Age:** 39  
**Location:** New York, NY  
**Tenure as CPO:** 18 months  

### Background
Priya spent eight years at a large fintech platform as a Senior PM, then Principal PM, before being recruited to Clarent to build its product function from scratch. She joined because she wanted to own something — not just contribute to someone else's roadmap. She is technically literate (can read code, understands architecture trade-offs), experienced in fintech compliance, and deeply customer-focused.

She now leads a team of 14 PMs and is sandwiched between a CEO who sends her AI articles three times a week and an engineering team that wants to experiment with everything. She has genuine conviction that AI will change her product category — she's just not sure which bets to make, in what order, and how to sequence them alongside the existing roadmap.

### Goals & Motivations
- Ship something meaningful with AI that actually improves customer outcomes — not just a feature for the press release
- Build a coherent AI product strategy she can defend upward (to the CEO and board) and downward (to engineering and design)
- Keep her team focused and motivated without burning them out on experiments that go nowhere
- Establish herself as the CPO who knows how to navigate the AI transition — a reputation she can carry forward in her career

### A Day in Priya's Life
**7:00am** — Reads Lenny's Newsletter over coffee. Today's issue covers AI product metrics. She takes notes on two frameworks she wants to adapt.  
**9:00am** — Sprint planning. Three tickets on the board are AI features requested by the CEO based on things he read. She quietly moves two of them to the backlog.  
**11:00am** — 1:1 with her lead engineer, who wants to integrate a new LLM API into the onboarding flow. She asks about latency, cost per call, and fallback handling. He doesn't have numbers yet.  
**12:30pm** — Slack message from the CEO: a LinkedIn article with *"Thoughts?"* This is the sixth one this month. She adds it to a folder she hasn't opened since March.  
**2:00pm** — Customer call. The customer asks what Clarent's "AI roadmap" looks like. Priya gives the "we're building toward some exciting capabilities" answer. She feels the same discomfort Marcus felt this morning but from the opposite direction.  
**4:00pm** — Writes a business case for an AI-powered reconciliation feature. Spends 45 minutes on the ROI section because she knows finance will ask for a payback period she can't honestly calculate yet.  
**7:30pm** — Opens Product Genesis. Reads two essays. Finds one specific case study she can reference in tomorrow's leadership meeting.

### Information Diet
- **Daily:** Lenny's Newsletter, Product Hunt, Twitter/X (follows AI researchers, product leaders, YC founders)
- **Weekly:** Product-Led Growth newsletter, a16z (selectively), First Round Review
- **Events:** Mind the Product, ProductCon, internal fintech industry briefings
- **Trusted voices:** Two former managers she still calls for career and strategy advice; a small Slack group of CPOs at similar-stage companies
- **How she consumes:** Primarily text; uses audio (podcasts: Lenny's Podcast, Masters of Scale) during workouts

### Decision-Making Style
Priya is data-driven but pragmatic. She builds structured business cases, runs small experiments before committing, and documents her reasoning. She is a strong writer — her PRDs are read as models by her team. She is also politically astute: she knows when to push back and when to absorb pressure and redirect it. She does not make large bets without a fallback.

### Relationship with AI — Current State
- Has shipped one AI feature (a smart search upgrade) that went well but was narrower than originally scoped
- Is running two AI experiments internally — one on customer onboarding, one on support routing — neither has conclusive results yet
- Reads broadly about AI product patterns but finds most content either too theoretical or too consumer-focused
- Worried about the gap between what vendors promise and what production reality looks like at her scale

### What She Needs from Content
- Real examples of AI product decisions — what was built, why, what the results were, what they'd do differently
- Honest coverage of failure modes — what breaks in production that looks fine in demos
- Frameworks for sequencing AI features alongside a non-AI roadmap under resource constraints
- Language she can borrow to communicate AI strategy to non-technical stakeholders

### Content Triggers — What Makes Her Click
- A title that names her exact situation ("how to build an AI roadmap when you have 18 months of existing commitments")
- A specific number or result she can test against her own context
- A practitioner byline — someone who actually shipped something, not a consultant or analyst
- A case study in fintech, payments, or adjacent B2B SaaS

### Content Killers — What Makes Her Bounce
- Thought leadership with no evidence
- Consumer-first examples with no relevance to B2B
- "Here's what GPT-4 can do" explainers aimed at beginners
- Any post that treats AI as purely a technology question rather than a product and organisational one

### Signature Quote
*"My CEO sends me every AI article he reads. My job is to figure out which 5% of it is actually true."*
