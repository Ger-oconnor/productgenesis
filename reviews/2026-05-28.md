### V1 · Note · The invisible shift: design's most important work is now its least visible
- [x] **Approve for publishing**
- **Source:** [The Most Important Part of Product Design Is Less Visible Now](https://medium.com/@gguasp1/the-most-important-part-of-product-design-is-less-visible-now-6b17785105c5) · Gretchen Guasp · Medium
- **Type:** note
- **Published:** May 2026
- **Why publish:** A sharp and specific practitioner observation about what AI is doing to design work at the level of what's valued vs. what's visible — complements the Designer Fund report with a more personal framing.
- **Suggested site title:** "AI executes the design. The part that still matters is the part nobody sees."
- **Suggested dek:** "The most important work in product design has always been upstream of execution: framing the right problem, setting direction, making the judgment call. AI is automating the visible part. What remains is harder to show in a portfolio."
- **Suggested tweet:** "AI handles the execution. The part of product design that matters most — framing the right problem, making the judgment call, setting direction — is the part that's always been invisible. It just got a lot harder to argue it should be well-paid. [link]"
- **Recommended post type:** note
- **Suggested read time:** 3 min

**Draft body:**

The most important work in product design has never been the Figma file. It has been the work that happens before the Figma file: deciding which problem to solve, framing it correctly, making the judgment call about what to build and why. That work is invisible in the output. It doesn't ship. It doesn't render. It doesn't appear in a portfolio.

AI is automating the visible layer of design — the prototyping, the variant generation, the layout exploration, the early ideation. In doing so, it is making the invisible layer more exposed. The designers who built their professional identity around craft execution are losing ground. The designers whose value was always in direction-setting and problem framing have more leverage than before. The distinction between these two groups was always present; AI has simply made it impossible to ignore.

For product teams, the implication is structural. If the most valuable design work is the work that precedes the tool — that cannot be prompted or generated — then the design process needs to start earlier and go deeper before it reaches the tools. Teams that use AI to go faster through discovery are compressing the most important part of the process. Teams that use AI to go further in execution while slowing down in framing are building the right advantage.

The distinction between an AI-assisted designer and an AI-native designer is not about which tools they use. It is about where they invest their cognitive effort. An AI-assisted designer uses AI to do design work faster. An AI-native designer has recognised that the design work worth doing has changed.

When AI produces solid starting points — and, for an increasing range of outputs, final results — the value of the designer's time shifts. Producing a prototype is no longer a significant investment. - **Recommended post type:** essay

**Draft body:**

LogRocket's May 2026 analysis lands a claim that is more pointed than the usual "AI is changing everything" framing: 2026 is the first year when product management must consciously evolve beyond the classical skill set. Not use AI to do the classical job faster. Actually evolve. The distinction matters because most PM teams are doing the first while thinking they're doing the second.

The first AI shockwave is engineering velocity compression. When development cycles shorten from weeks to days, the ratio of time spent in discovery versus delivery inverts. PM teams built for the old cadence — long sprints, careful requirements documentation, sequential delivery — are running a process that's now mismatched to the execution speed beneath them. The planning horizon for a feature that ships in a day is different from one that ships in a quarter. Most PM processes haven't been redesigned for the new cadence.

The second shockwave is user behaviour change at machine speed. AI products generate behavioural data faster and at greater volume than any previous software category. Users adapt quickly. Engagement patterns shift. Failure modes emerge and correct rapidly. The classical PM practice of quarterly user research and biannual roadmap updates is structurally too slow for a product whose user relationship changes in weeks. The PM role in 2026 requires a continuous intelligence loop, not a periodic one.

The third shockwave is what LogRocket calls autonomous product learning loops — AI features that modify their own behaviour based on usage data without a PM initiating the change. This creates a new challenge: how do you set the intent and constraints for a product that is self-modifying? The PM's job in this context is no longer decision-by-decision product direction. It is defining the values, goals, and limits that govern the product's autonomous decisions. That is a different kind of work — closer to governance than to traditional product management.

The practical implication is that the PM role in 2026 is a hybrid of strategist, systems thinker, and AI-literate builder. The teams that recognise this are redesigning their processes, their rituals, and their skill sets accordingly. The teams that haven't recognised it are running the 2022 playbook in 2026, which is not the same as running it slowly. It is running the wrong playbook.

- **Suggested tweet:** "Two GTM motions are working for AI startups in 2026: bottom-up PLG (Cursor, Lovable, ElevenLabs) and top-down enterprise sales (Harvey, Sierra, Glean, Decagon). The moat in both cases is the same: data that accumulates with use. Everything else gets competed away. [link]"
- **Recommended post type:** note
- **Suggested read time:** 3 min

**Draft body:**

The AI startup landscape in 2026 has clarified around two GTM motions that are demonstrably working. Bottom-up, product-led growth — the motion used by Cursor, Lovable, Replit, and ElevenLabs — scales through free tiers, viral loops, and community adoption. It works when the product generates immediate individual value and when users can become advocates before any enterprise sale is made. Top-down enterprise sales — the motion used by Harvey, Sierra, Glean, Decagon, and Abridge — scales through design partners, structured pilot programmes, and direct relationship-building with economic buyers. It works when the value is in deeply embedding AI into regulated or complex workflows where individual use doesn't capture the full opportunity.

The real moats in both models are the same. Proprietary data that accumulates from real usage — training signal, feedback loops, domain knowledge — is the advantage that competitors cannot replicate by accessing the same foundation models. Deep workflow embedding through integrations and habit creates switching costs that make AI products stickier than any SaaS product in the previous era. Distribution and brand at the category level determine who gets called first when an enterprise buyer defines a new initiative. And user state — the context, history, and preferences that live inside the product and that represent irreplaceable memory — creates an exit cost that grows with time.

The timing observation that runs across analysis of which startups are succeeding in 2026 is consistent: the companies raising large rounds and closing significant enterprise deals are the ones that locked in early customers and started generating proprietary data during 2024 and 2025. Enterprise buyers are getting more disciplined. The startups that enter 2026 without an established customer base and data advantage are competing for a narrower window of opportunity than their counterparts from a year earlier. The AI startup opportunity is real and large — but it is increasingly winner-takes-most within verticals rather than open to all.
