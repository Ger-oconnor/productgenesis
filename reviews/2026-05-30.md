- **Recommended post type:** note

**Draft body:**

A year ago, the idea that AI would fundamentally reshape design tooling was still aspirational. What Onlook observes in May 2026 is that error cases — the frustrating failures that made AI-assisted design feel experimental — have almost entirely stopped happening. Agent workflows have matured with Claude Code and Codex. Models provide more reliable output. Designers have learned what it is reasonable to ask for. The experimental phase is over. This is the baseline now.

The implication for designers is structural. When the canvas is sourced in code by default, exportable and responsive without additional work, and the spec can be pulled from one tool and cross-referenced against engineering work in another, the design medium has changed. The question is no longer whether AI can help. It is whether a designer's judgment, taste, and craft can stay ahead of what an LLM will suggest as the obvious first answer. If a designer's value-add does not exceed the LLM's default output, the tool makes that visible very quickly.

The most pointed observation in the piece concerns the bar for UX decisions. The "obvious" choices that used to differentiate competent designers from juniors — clear hierarchy, predictable affordances, sensible defaults — are now one prompt away. That is not an argument for lower standards. It is an argument that the floor of design competence just rose, and designers who were trading on their ability to produce those obvious answers have less professional edge than they had twelve months ago.

- **Suggested dek:** "It is not that PMs use more AI tools — it is that the job itself has changed. Some tasks dropped to near-zero cost. Others became more judgment-intensive. - **Suggested tweet:** "Agentic AI hasn't just added tools to the PM's workflow — it's changed which tasks are worth doing at all. Some work dropped to near-zero effort. - **Recommended post type:** note
- **Suggested read time:** 3 min

**Draft body:**

The product manager's job in 2026 has not just gained new tools — it has changed in structure. Agentic AI compresses the cost of certain tasks to near zero: synthesis of user research, competitive analysis, first-draft documentation, initial backlog grooming, and routine data analysis can all be handled by agents at the PM's direction. This does not make PMs more productive at the same job. It changes which parts of the job constitute the actual work.

What remains high-judgment for PMs in 2026 is the set of decisions where AI gives confident, plausible, and wrong answers. Strategic prioritisation — choosing which problem to solve and why, given the specific competitive and customer context of a particular product — requires context and stakes-awareness that agents lack. Stakeholder navigation, deciding what to escalate and how to frame it, is still human work. And the evaluation of AI-generated outputs — knowing when the synthesis is off, when the feature spec misses the real user need, when the roadmap looks coherent but optimises for the wrong thing — requires the same deep product judgment that the best PMs have always needed, now applied to reviewing outputs rather than producing them.

The risk the piece identifies is specific: the tasks where PMs are most likely to defer to AI are the ones that look like synthesis but are actually strategic framing. When an agent produces a summary of user research or a feature prioritisation recommendation, it looks complete and reads as authoritative. The PM who takes that output at face value without applying judgment to the framing and assumptions is not saving time — they are delegating a judgment call to a system not equipped to make it.

**Draft body:**

Podcast hosts talk about $300-per-day AI agents, and according to Bryan Catanzaro, VP of Applied Deep Learning at Nvidia, "For my team, the cost of compute is far beyond the costs of the employees." CIO's May 27 analysis puts specific examples on the claim: individual engineers are running monthly token bills north of $150,000. Engineers running Claude Code through Uber's API burned through the company's entire 2026 AI budget in four months. A Stockholm-based software engineer reported spending more on Claude per month than their own salary. These are not edge-case warnings — they are documented examples from mainstream enterprise deployments of tools that are becoming standard in development workflows.

The structural reason for the cost problem is architectural. Software engineers sometimes run multiple AI agents simultaneously on different background tasks without supervision. Each accumulates tokens — and agentic systems are not efficient consumers of tokens by design. According to Gartner's March 2026 analysis, agentic models require between 5 and 30 times more tokens per task than a standard generative AI chatbot. An agentic workflow may trigger 10 to 20 LLM calls to complete a single user-initiated task. Without a token budget, a model routing policy, and per-user cost attribution, the economics of a development tool become the economics of an unmonitored cloud service — growth that compounds weekly until someone sees the invoice.

Microsoft's 2026 Cyber Pulse survey quantifies the readiness gap: 80% of Fortune 500 companies have deployed AI agents, but only 47% have agent-specific security and cost policies in place. That 33-point gap between deployment and governance is a current operating condition, not a future risk. The practical checklist for operations leaders is straightforward: token usage tagging per team, a model routing policy that sends low-complexity queries to cheaper models, per-user cost alerts at 50% and 80% of budget thresholds, and monthly reviews that attribute AI spend to business outcomes rather than treating it as overhead. The teams running these controls now are not over-managing their AI stack. They are building the cost infrastructure that makes continued AI investment defensible to CFOs.

**Draft body:**

OpenAI published its Frontier Governance Framework on May 29, 2026, as a public document that maps the company's internal safety and security practices to two specific regulatory frameworks: California's Transparency in Frontier AI Act and the EU AI Act's Code of Practice for General Purpose AI. The framework covers five areas: risk assessment and mitigation across cyber offense, CBRN risks, harmful manipulation, and loss of control; model reporting; security risk management; incident response; and external expert input. OpenAI's internal Preparedness Framework remains the foundation, with the Frontier Governance Framework translating its most relevant provisions into the regulatory compliance language that enterprise legal and governance teams can use.

For enterprise operations leaders managing AI governance programs, the significance of this document is practical. Governance programs that include OpenAI models now have a provider-side reference that explains what controls OpenAI maintains at the model level. The EU AI Act's Code of Practice for General Purpose AI includes obligations on the model provider that sit upstream of the obligations of the enterprise deployer. Understanding where the provider's obligations end and the enterprise team's begin is the boundary question that compliance programs need answered before August 2026, when high-risk AI system obligations under the EU AI Act become fully applicable. The Frontier Governance Framework provides OpenAI's answer to that question.

The framework is also a competitive signal. Anthropic has its Responsible Scaling Policy. Google has Gemini governance documentation. The publication of governance frameworks is becoming a baseline criterion for enterprise AI procurement — providers that cannot produce a public governance document are increasingly disadvantaged in enterprise sales conversations, regardless of model performance benchmarks. For operations teams evaluating model provider relationships, governance documentation is now a procurement criterion alongside capability, pricing, and SLA terms. The question to ask is not whether the framework exists, but whether the obligations it describes are auditable and whether the enterprise's own governance layer correctly allocates responsibility between provider and deployer.
